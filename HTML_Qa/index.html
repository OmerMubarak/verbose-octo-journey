Learning About Llamas.

Origins

The llama (/ˈlɑːmə/; Spanish pronunciation: [ˈʎama]) (Lama glama) is a domesticated South American camelid, widely used as a meat and pack animal by Andean cultures since the Pre-Columbian era.

Medical uses

Doctors and researches have determined that llamas possess antibodies that are well suited to treat certain diseases.[37] Scientists have been studying the way llamas might contribute to the fight against coronaviruses, including MERS and SARS-CoV-2 (which causes COVID-19).

Nutrition

Options for feeding llamas are quite wide; a wide variety of commercial and farm-based feeds are available.

Mating

Llamas mate with in a kush (lying down) position, which is fairly unusual in a large animal. They mate for an extended time (20–45 minutes), also unusual in a large animal.

Fiber

Llamas have a fine undercoat, which can be used for handicrafts and garments. The coarser outer guard hair is used for rugs, wall-hangings and lead ropes. The fiber comes in many different colors ranging from white or grey to reddish-brown, brown, dark brown and black.

Characteristics

A full-grown llama can reach a height of 1.7 to 1.8 m (5 ft 7 in to 5 ft 11 in) at the top of the head, and can weigh between 130 and 200 kg (290 and 440 lb).

At birth, a baby llama (called a cria) can weigh between 9 and 14 kg (20 and 31 lb).

Life Span

Llamas typically live for 15 to 25 years, with some individuals surviving 30 years or more.

Resemblance to other animals

The skull generally resembles that of Camelus, the larger brain-cavity and orbits, and less-developed cranial ridges being due to its smaller size.
